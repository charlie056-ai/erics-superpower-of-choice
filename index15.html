Welcome to the Hall of Parents

Before you enter, take a moment. Place your hand over your heart. Read these words — and mean them.

I will remember that my child is not giving me a hard time — they are having a hard time.

I will look beneath the behavior before I react to it.

I will respect my child's timeline for change — growth cannot be rushed, only supported.

I will use fewer words when feelings are big, and more patience when progress feels small.

I will repair after hard moments — because going back and making it right teaches more than getting it right the first time.

I will model what I ask of my child — calm, honesty, accountability, and the courage to try again.

I will ask before I assume — "Help me understand what is hard right now" — before I correct, lecture, or consequence.

I will believe that my child is not broken. They are learning. And so am I.

I will choose connection over control — because a child who feels connected wants to try harder for you.

📖 How to use Parent Mode
Start with Understanding Your Child — it reframes everything and changes how the other tools land.

Then try What to Say — pick one phrase and practice it before your next hard moment.

Use Family Scenarios alone first, then try them with your child. Compare answers. You might be surprised.

If you and your partner are not on the same page — there is a section specifically for that inside Parent Mode.

Come back to Repair any time something goes sideways. It always can be fixed.

This pledge is not a promise of perfection. It is a commitment to keep showing up — even after hard days, hard words, and hard moments.

When Your Partner Feels Left Behind

One of the most common — and least talked about — dynamics in parenting growth.

When one parent begins to grow — becoming calmer, more connected, more intentional — the other parent can feel unsettled in ways they cannot quite name.

It is rarely about the parenting technique itself. It is usually about fear.

A spouse may be asking questions they haven't said out loud yet:
"Am I falling behind? Do they think I'm doing it wrong? What is my place now?"

They may be hearing "you need to be better" — even when their partner is simply saying "I learned something that helped me."

That is not weakness. That is fear showing up dressed as stubbornness, arguments, or silence.

What tends to help

1
Start with appreciation — not a lesson.
Before sharing a new idea, say something you genuinely appreciate about your partner. They need to feel like your teammate, not your student.
2
Ask — don't tell.
"Would you be willing to try one small thing with me?" People open up when they feel respected and free. They shut down when they feel cornered or graded.
3
Ask for one small experiment — not a whole new system.
Just one small change for three days. Then decide together whether it helped. Small and specific wins every time over big and overwhelming.
4
Notice every good-faith effort — out loud.
When your partner tries — even a little — tell them you noticed. Specifically. Those small moments of feeling seen are what slowly build trust between two people learning something new together.
"You cannot want this enough for both of you. You can plant seeds — but you cannot make it rain. What you can do is keep growing — quietly, steadily, with grace — and invite your partner along without dragging them. The goal is to walk beside them, not ahead of them with a report card."
And one more thing — because it matters
If your partner uses your growth as a reason to humiliate you, control you, or make you feel small — that is no longer just a communication problem. That is something different, and you would not have to face it alone.

But if this is simply two people learning how to dance to new music — be patient. Be kind. Be brave enough to go first.

Growth in one partner is always an invitation to the other.

There Is No Timeline

A note from someone who has spent 30 years watching families grow.

Let me be honest about something I saw again and again over 30 years of working with families.

One of the most common questions parents ask is: "How long will it take for my child to change?"

That question is understandable. When a family is struggling, parents naturally want relief. They want progress. They want to know that the effort they are putting in will make a difference.

But here is the truth: growth does not happen on a fixed timeline.

And more importantly, the responsibility for change should never be placed entirely on the child.

Children do not arrive at difficult behaviors by accident. Family patterns develop over time. They are shaped by history, stress, expectations, communication styles, reactions, habits, and sometimes pain that has never been fully understood.

"If blame were the goal, there would usually be enough to go around. But blame is not the goal. Growth is."
The Dog Training Truth
In some ways, this process is not very different from what happens in a dog obedience class.

Many owners arrive believing the dog is the problem. But very often, the trainer is watching the owner just as closely — their timing, tone, consistency, body language, and ability to follow through.

In other words, the dog is not the only one being trained.

Parenting works in a similar way. These tools are not only about helping your child change. They are also about helping the adults in the home become more aware, more consistent, more patient, and more intentional.

That is not a criticism. It is the heart of the work. When the adult in the room begins to shift, the child often begins to shift too.
We Are All a Work in Progress
The idea of perfect parenting is an illusion. Not because parents are failing, but because perfection was never the right goal.

A healthier goal is awareness. Awareness allows us to recognize what is working, what is not working, and what we may need to adjust in ourselves as we continue growing.

You are not expected to become a perfect parent. You are being invited to become a more conscious one.

And your child is not expected to become a perfect child. They are being given the opportunity to learn, practice, repair, and grow.

That is where real change begins.
A Word for Those Who Are Not Sure They Can Do This
There are those who will read these words and feel something quiet inside them — a voice that says "this sounds good, but I don't think I'm capable of it."

If that is you, I want you to hear this.

Michelangelo once said that the sculpture already existed inside the stone. His job was simply to remove what was hiding it.

You are no different.

The capable, growing, loving parent you are looking for is not someone you have to become from scratch. They are already there — waiting beneath the noise, the exhaustion, the self-doubt, and the old patterns that no longer fit who you are becoming.

Trust that quiet, gentle voice within. Find a moment of stillness. Go somewhere where quietude reigns, lend your ear upon your heart — and just listen.

What you hear there will not mislead you.
Let Go of the Clock
Try not to measure your child's progress by a calendar. Try not to compare your family's growth to someone else's. Try not to place an invisible deadline on change and then feel discouraged when life does not follow it.

Growth rarely happens on demand. It happens through patience, repetition, repair, consistency, and the quiet decision to keep showing up — even when the progress feels slow.

You are not a failure because change is taking time. You are a parent doing difficult work.
"Parents who keep trying — even imperfectly, even slowly, even on the hard days — are giving their child steadiness, hope, and a model of what it means to keep growing.

So let go of the clock. Keep showing up. That is not everything, but it is enough for today."

Repair is possible. Always.

"When you go back and repair — you teach your child something they will carry for life."

The Waterfront Moment

We underestimate what children are already carrying inside

The Experience
I was five or six years old. My grandparents were watching a movie — On the Waterfront — and somehow I was in the room.

Marlon Brando, in that gruff Brooklyn voice, delivered a line that stopped me cold:

"I coulda had class. I coulda been a contender. I could've been somebody — instead of a bum, which is what I am."

I felt something. I could not have named it then. But I felt it — deep and clear — as compassion for that man on the screen. And somewhere in that small chest of mine, a quiet promise formed: I will not go down that road.

Now — what does a five or six year old know about contenders and bums and the roads that lead between them? According to conventional wisdom: nothing. Children that age have not accumulated enough life experience to reason in those terms.

But I did. Maybe not in words. But in something more fundamental than words. And I suspect I was not alone.

💎 The Treasure
Do not underestimate the child in the room. They are already thinking. They are already feeling. They are already deciding — in ways they cannot yet articulate — who they want to become.

Your job is not to fill an empty vessel. Your job is to be worth paying attention to.
Tools this story demonstrates:
💭 Children process emotions earlier and deeper than we think
⚡ The Thinking Theorem begins in childhood — before it has a name
👀 What children witness shapes who they become
🌱 Adults are always teaching — whether they mean to or not

Stroke of Pluck

A true story about calm, panic, and a stranger who moved toward danger

December 1963
How great would it be if we brought home a duck for Christmas dinner?

Once again, we had found an opportunity to fine-tune our lack of impulse control.

We moved closer. Willis grabbed his oar and took a swing at one of the ducks — which was sitting just far enough away to avoid being knocked into next Tuesday. But that sudden movement was all it took.

The boat shifted. The balance changed. The little dinghy tilted sharply. Before I could catch myself, I went over the side and into the freezing water.

Within moments, water rushed into the boat. The good news was that the boat was still floating. The bad news was that it was floating below the surface.

We were both wearing heavy winter clothes — double pants, heavy shirts, boots, and jackets. In six feet of freezing water, those clothes quickly became dead weight.

I had heard people talk about panic before, but I had never truly seen it take over a person until that moment. Willis began screaming, flailing, grabbing at me, and pulling me under with him.

I grabbed him and said, "I got you."

I thought those words might calm him. But fear had already taken hold of him. Panic does not listen to reason. It does not stop to think. It only reacts. The more I tried to help, the more he pulled at me. Soon he was pulling both of us under.

I had no choice. I pushed him away from me and toward the boat. "Hold on to it. Just touch it gently. Relax. We can work our way back."

But I learned something that day that stayed with me for life: when a person is trapped inside panic, words alone may not reach them.

I refused to leave my brother. But I also knew that if I let him grab me again, we might both go down. So I stayed close enough to help him, but far enough away to keep him from pulling me under.

The cold should have been unbearable, but at first I barely felt it. My adrenaline was racing so hard it seemed to block out everything else. Beneath one thought: Keep Willis alive.

Minute after minute passed. I looked toward the road and saw people gathering. They were pointing at us, their faces filled with worry. They could see we were in trouble. But no one moved.

Then I saw movement to my left.

A man named Robert A. Gring — a retired Navy officer — was driving home from church with his wife when he noticed our predicament. He drove as close to the pond as he could, jumped out of his car, and moved with the kind of decisiveness that only courage can produce. He removed his jacket. He handed his belongings to his wife. He kicked off his shoes. Then he dove into the freezing water.

He did not have time to ask whose idea it was or why two boys were out there. He simply saw human beings in danger and moved toward them.

When he reached us, he looked at me.

"Get my brother," I told him. "I'm okay."

Mr. Gring grabbed Willis, shook him, and had to slap him to bring him back into the here and now. Finally, Willis began to calm down enough to be rescued. Somehow, through cold, fear, struggle, and grace, we made it out of that pond alive.

I have never forgotten Robert A. Gring. He was a stranger to us — but in that critical moment, he became the difference between life and death.

💎 The Treasure
Adventure without judgment can become danger.
Panic can pull more than one person under.
Calm gives you a chance to survive.
And compassion, when joined with courage, can save a life.

Sometimes, the strongest person is not the one who feels no fear.
Sometimes, the strongest person is the one who sees fear, feels the danger, and still moves toward someone who needs help.

The Deli Story

When to use humor — and when to shift gears

The Experience
I was 17 years old, working in the deli department of a big box supermarket. My girlfriend was a cashier, and she had introduced me to her friend Alicia. The three of us would take breaks together — cracking jokes, chatting, laughing. Nothing more than that.

One evening I noticed Alicia's boyfriend lurking around the grocery aisles. And trailing behind him was a friend — a lineman I remembered from football. Six foot four. Over 200 pounds of mostly muscle.

I read the body language immediately. I took a break from my department and walked straight toward them.

Alicia's boyfriend started making accusations — convinced something was going on between his girlfriend and me. He was working himself into a lather. I stayed calm and told him there was nothing going on. We just liked to hang out and laugh.

It wasn't landing. So I shifted gears. I turned to John — the big friend — and asked if he and I could talk alone for a moment. He could have crushed me. But I asked anyway. He agreed.

"Look — I know my odds of beating you in a fight are slim to perhaps none. And I'll fight if there's no other alternative. But either way it's a lose-lose proposition. We both walk away with bruises — and for what? I have a girlfriend. I like Alicia as a friend. Nothing more."

John nodded slowly. Then: "I'll try to talk some sense into my friend." That was the last I heard of it.

💎 The Treasure
There is a time to be humorous and a time to shift gears. Knowing which one the moment calls for — and trusting your inner voice enough to act on it — is one of the most important skills a person can develop. It cannot be taught in a classroom. It grows through experience, self-awareness, and the willingness to take a risk on connection over conflict.

The Plumber Story

Why the size of your toolbox matters

The Experience
Imagine two plumbers — Tom and Charlie.

Tom shows up carrying only one pair of pliers. The job is bigger than expected. A leak, a loose pipe, a stuck valve, a part that needs replacing. Tom tries his best — but one pair of pliers cannot fix every problem. Tom is not a bad plumber. He simply does not have enough tools for the job.

Now imagine Charlie. He arrives with a truck full of tools. When he sees the same problem, he has options. If one tool does not work, he tries another.
💎 The Treasure
People do not struggle because they are broken. They struggle because they are missing a tool. Every new skill, habit, sentence, or strategy a person learns becomes another option in a hard moment. The size of your toolbox often influences the quality of your choices.

The Counselor Story

Four words that changed everything

The Experience
One important communication lesson came from learning a single phrase that changed the tone of difficult conversations for good.

"Would you be willing to...?"

Instead of pushing, demanding, or trying to control — it invited the other person to consider a choice. The shift was small. The effect was powerful.

When someone hears a demand, they may resist, shut down, or feel controlled. When someone hears a respectful question, they often feel more dignity — and more room to cooperate.

💎 The Treasure
Respectful invitation is almost always more effective than force. "Would you be willing to try again?" "Would you be willing to take a breath with me?" "Would you be willing to tell me what happened?" The person still has a choice — and that choice matters.

Who was really being trained?

The Experience
Dog owners often arrive at obedience training believing the dog is the problem. They want the barking to stop, the jumping to stop, the pulling on the leash to stop.

But during training, something important becomes clear — the trainer is watching the owner just as closely as the dog. Their timing. Their tone. Their consistency. Their follow-through.

The owner begins to realize: the dog is not the only one being trained.
💎 The Treasure

The Monster Story

What happens when you face what scares you

The Experience
Childhood nightmares about monsters can feel very real. In the dream, the monster chases, threatens, and seems too frightening to face. Running away feels like the only option.

The Four-Step Repair Story

How middle schoolers taught us about healing

The Experience
A conflict-resolution process was used with middle school students to help resolve ongoing conflicts and restore relationships. Many student conflicts continued because each person felt unheard or judged. The Four-Step Repair Process created a structure where both students could be heard before trying to repair the relationship.
1
Tell Your Story
Person A shares what happened, how they felt, and why they were upset.
2
Show Me You Heard Me
Person B paraphrases — "What I hear you saying is..." — before responding.
3
Switch Roles
Person B speaks. Person A listens and paraphrases.
4
Repair and Reconnect
Both discuss responsibility, repair, forgiveness, and moving forward.
💎 The Treasure
Feeling heard often reduces anger and opens the door to repair. Understanding does not require agreement. Understanding usually comes before healing.

⭐Charlie's Story

Why This Project Was Created

Eric's Superpower of Choice

This page shares a personal story, including the loss of a loved one. We share it because it is part of why this project exists.

The inspiration for this project came from my two grandsons, who I knew were struggling — Eric and Vinny. Both faced similar behavioral challenges, and both often became defined by those behaviors in the eyes of others.

But to understand why this project exists, it helps to know a little about where I came from.

Where I came from
I did not grow up in ideal conditions. My mother was a young single parent of three, doing everything she could with what she had. We learned early to take care of ourselves — make the bed, pour the cereal, catch the bus. We lived in a modest neighborhood of mixed races and chose our friends by how well we got along, not by the color of their skin.

For a stretch of years I lived with my Aunt Janet and Uncle Lenny — two of the finest people I have ever known. They showed me what a stable, loving, values-driven home looked like. That mattered more than I can say.

What I learned from all of it — the chaos and the stability, the struggle and the grace — is that children are far more resilient, far more perceptive, and far more capable than most adults give them credit for.

I learned to listen to something inside me at a very young age. Not because someone taught me to. Because there was often nobody else in the room.

That inner voice has never led me wrong. It is the same voice this framework asks every parent, every child, and every educator to find.

But through both my professional and personal life, I learned that behavior is rarely the whole story.

Children do not wake up wanting to struggle. What we see on the outside often reflects something happening inside — fear, frustration, confusion, unmet needs, or emotions they cannot yet explain.

I spent my career supporting children as a special education educator, counselor, and assistant principal responsible for discipline. Those experiences taught me a great deal. But being a grandparent taught me something deeper.

Watching someone you love struggle changes you. Eric became one of the deepest reasons this project exists. His journey reminded me that children need more than correction — they need understanding, practical tools, and adults willing to look beneath the behavior.

Vinny shaped this project too. He went through years of pain that, from the outside, often looked like "just behavior." Looking back, I believe what was really happening was a child carrying feelings too big to name, with no clear lifeline to reach for.

His story reinforced something I had already learned: behavior, emotions, thoughts, and choices matter deeply — and children need adults who are willing to notice what may be happening beneath the surface, long before things become unbearable.

Children need understanding. They need tools. They need connection. They need hope.

But Eric and Vinny's stories are not the only experiences that shaped me.

Forty-five years earlier, I experienced the most excruciating pain of my life when I lost my younger brother, Morris. I had enlisted in the Army and was less than a week from graduating boot camp when another recruit approached me and said the Sergeant Major wanted to speak with me. When I entered the orderly room, Sergeant Gonzalez asked if anyone in my family was sick. I said no. He told me to call home.

In that moment, life changed.

Life has a way of placing us at crossroads. There are crushing events and difficult seasons that can leave us feeling helpless. But there is always a choice in how we respond. One path leads toward bitterness, silence, and surrender. The other leads toward meaning, healing, hope, and possibility.

This project became my choice.

Eric's Superpower of Choice was created to help children recognize what is happening inside themselves — before emotions become actions, and before actions become consequences.

Throughout my career, I had the privilege of working with parents in groups and teaching parenting skills. Again and again, I witnessed how much parents care, and how often they simply need encouragement, understanding, and practical tools.

It is my hope that the lessons shared throughout this site become a source of support for families — to help create homes with less chaos, more connection, and more fun.

Choices are not random. They begin inside us.

A body signal becomes a feeling. A feeling becomes a thought. A thought becomes a choice. And repeated choices become the direction of a life.

This project does not claim to have all the answers. It is an invitation — for children, parents, educators, and caregivers to pause, to notice, to become curious, and to ask: What is happening inside me right now?

Because every child has strengths and deserves the chance to understand themselves. And perhaps their greatest superpower is learning they can influence what happens next.

If this project helps even one child feel seen, understood, or find another path forward — then this work, and every patch in my quilt, will continue to matter.

A final note — about who built this

I have never been a great orator. I played guitar and sang — and I loved it — but I know my limitations with my voice and my guitar skills. Not from a lack of trying. I simply learned, over time, to perform an honest inventory of the talents I am blessed with.

I have gratitude for what I have been given. And I choose not to carry jealousy or resentment for the talents I wish I had but do not. That is not resignation. That is peace.

I have also had issues with short-term memory for as long as I can remember. It is not something I need to be psychoanalyzed about. It is simply something I grew into accepting. The payoff has been a sense of humor I might not have found otherwise, and a level of peacefulness within that I can genuinely bask in.

I am, admittedly, a bit wordy. But my prose is a gift — and it is my voice. And this site is what happens when that voice finally found something worth saying.

— Charlie
