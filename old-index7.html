⭐Parent Mode

Welcome to the Hall of Parents

Before you enter, take a moment. Place your hand over your heart. Read these words — and mean them.

I will remember that my child is not giving me a hard time — they are having a hard time.

I will look beneath the behavior before I react to it.

I will respect my child's timeline for change — growth cannot be rushed, only supported.

I will use fewer words when feelings are big, and more patience when progress feels small.

I will repair after hard moments — because going back and making it right teaches more than getting it right the first time.

I will model what I ask of my child — calm, honesty, accountability, and the courage to try again.

I will ask before I assume — "Help me understand what is hard right now" — before I correct, lecture, or consequence.

I will believe that my child is not broken. They are learning. And so am I.

I will choose connection over control — because a child who feels connected wants to try harder for you.

This pledge is not a promise of perfection. It is a commitment to keep showing up — even after hard days, hard words, and hard moments.

When Your Partner Feels Left Behind

One of the most common — and least talked about — dynamics in parenting growth.

When one parent begins to grow — becoming calmer, more connected, more intentional — the other parent can feel unsettled in ways they cannot quite name.

It is rarely about the parenting technique itself. It is usually about fear.

A spouse may be asking questions they haven't said out loud yet:
"Am I falling behind? Do they think I'm doing it wrong? What is my place now?"

They may be hearing "you need to be better" — even when their partner is simply saying "I learned something that helped me."

That is not weakness. That is fear showing up dressed as stubbornness, arguments, or silence.

What tends to help

1
Start with appreciation — not a lesson.
Before sharing a new idea, say something you genuinely appreciate about your partner. They need to feel like your teammate, not your student.
2
Ask — don't tell.
"Would you be willing to try one small thing with me?" People open up when they feel respected and free. They shut down when they feel cornered or graded.
3
Ask for one small experiment — not a whole new system.
Just one small change for three days. Then decide together whether it helped. Small and specific wins every time over big and overwhelming.
4
Notice every good-faith effort — out loud.
When your partner tries — even a little — tell them you noticed. Specifically. Those small moments of feeling seen are what slowly build trust between two people learning something new together.
"You cannot want this enough for both of you. You can plant seeds — but you cannot make it rain. What you can do is keep growing — quietly, steadily, with grace — and invite your partner along without dragging them. The goal is to walk beside them, not ahead of them with a report card."
And one more thing — because it matters
If your partner uses your growth as a reason to humiliate you, control you, or make you feel small — that is no longer just a communication problem. That is something different, and you would not have to face it alone.

But if this is simply two people learning how to dance to new music — be patient. Be kind. Be brave enough to go first.

Growth in one partner is always an invitation to the other.

Repair is possible. Always.

"When you go back and repair — you teach your child something they will carry for life."
1
Wait until you are both calm.
Repair cannot happen in the middle of the storm.
2
Name what happened honestly.
"I got really frustrated and I said something that was not fair to you. I am sorry."
3
Listen to their experience.
"How did that feel for you?" Then just listen. Do not defend.
4
Reconnect.
"I love you. We are okay. Let us try again."

⭐Child Repair
🔧

Everybody makes mistakes.

What makes someone strong is knowing how to make things better after something goes wrong.

"You are not your mistake. You are what you do next."
1
Calm down first.
You cannot repair when your feelings are still really big.
2
Say what happened.
"I said something mean." Say it simply and honestly.
3
Say you are sorry.
"I am sorry I did that." A real sorry — because you mean it.
4
Ask how to make it better.
"What can I do to help fix it?" or "Are we okay?"
⭐Teen Repair
🔧

Repair is not weakness. It is one of the hardest things a person can do.

"An apology is words. Repair is action. Both matter — but repair matters more."
1
Wait until you are both calm.
Repair attempted in the middle of a fight almost always fails.
2
Own what you did — without excuses.
"I said something really hurtful." The but cancels the repair.
3
Listen to how it landed for them.
"How did that feel for you?" Then actually listen. Do not defend.
4
Ask what would help and follow through.
"What do you need from me right now?" Then do what you say.
⭐Educator Repair
🔧

Even the best educators have hard moments.

"When an educator repairs with a student — they model something that student may never have seen before."
Repairing with a student:
"Earlier I handled that in a way that was not fair to you. I am sorry. You did not deserve that."
Repairing with a whole class:
"Before we start today I want to name something. Yesterday did not go the way I wanted. Let us start fresh."
Repairing with a parent:
"I wanted to follow up on our last conversation. I want to make sure we are on the same team for your child."

Get the most out of every visit

Whether you are a child, a teen, a parent, or a teacher — here is how to make this site work best for you.

1
Start with WHO YOU ARE
The home page has four doors — Child, Teen, Parent, and Educator. Each one is written specifically for that audience. Start there first. Everything inside is designed for your age group and your role.
2
Explore your tools one at a time
Inside each section you will find feelings check-ins, calm-down strategies, real-life scenarios, and repair guides. You do not have to do everything at once. Pick one tool and try it.
3
Use the Practice scenarios slowly
The scenarios work best when you slow down and really think about each choice — not just pick the "right" answer. There are no wrong answers here. Every choice teaches you something.
4
Try Repair after a hard moment
If something went wrong between you and someone you care about, the Repair section gives you a clear path back. It works for kids, teens, parents, and teachers alike.
5
Come back often
This is not a one-time read. The tools get stronger the more you use them. A child might do the feelings check-in every day. A parent might revisit the language guide before a tough conversation.
6
Families can use it together
Parents and children can go through the scenarios together, compare answers, and talk about what they would really do. This works especially well as a dinner table conversation or a car ride activity.

Share your thoughts when you are done

⭐Charlie's Story

Why This Project Was Created

Eric's Superpower of Choice

This page shares a personal story, including the loss of a loved one. We share it because it's part of why this project exists.

The inspiration for this project came from my two grandsons, who I knew were struggling — Eric and Vinny. Both faced similar behavioral challenges, and both often became defined by those behaviors in the eyes of others.

But through both my professional and personal life, I learned that behavior is rarely the whole story.

Children do not wake up wanting to struggle. What we see on the outside often reflects something happening inside — fear, frustration, confusion, unmet needs, or emotions they cannot yet explain.

I spent my career supporting children as a special education educator, counselor, and assistant principal responsible for discipline. Those experiences taught me a great deal. But being a grandparent taught me something deeper.

Watching someone you love struggle changes you. Eric became one of the deepest reasons this project exists. His journey reminded me that children need more than correction — they need understanding, practical tools, and adults willing to look beneath the behavior.

Vinny shaped this project too.

He went through years of pain that, from the outside, often looked like "just behavior." Looking back, I believe what was really happening was a child carrying feelings too big to name, with no clear lifeline to reach for — no adult who quite understood what was happening beneath the surface, and no tools for finding the way back to solid ground.

His story reinforced something I had already learned through years of working with children: behavior, emotions, thoughts, and choices matter deeply — and children need adults who are willing to notice what may be happening beneath the surface, long before things become unbearable.

Children need understanding. They need tools. They need connection. They need hope.

But Eric and Vinny's stories are not the only experiences that shaped me.

Forty-five years earlier, I experienced the most excruciating pain of my life when I lost my younger brother, Morris. I had enlisted in the Army and was less than a week from graduating boot camp when another recruit approached me and said the Sergeant Major wanted to speak with me. When I entered the orderly room, Sergeant Gonzalez asked if anyone in my family was sick. I said no. He told me to call home.

In that moment, life changed.

Life has a way of placing us at crossroads. There are crushing events and difficult seasons that can leave us feeling helpless. But there is always a choice in how we respond. One path leads toward bitterness, silence, and surrender. The other leads toward meaning, healing, hope, and possibility. That crossroad is never very far away — and one of our greatest life-saving tools is the power of choice.

This project became my choice.

Eric's Superpower of Choice was created to help children recognize what is happening inside themselves — before emotions become actions, and before actions become consequences.

But this project also serves another purpose. It was created to support and empower parents as they take on one of the most important — and often most difficult — roles they will ever have.

Throughout my career, I had the privilege of working with parents in groups and teaching parenting skills. Again and again, I witnessed how much parents care, and how often they simply need encouragement, understanding, and practical tools. It is my hope that the lessons shared throughout this site become a source of support for families — to help create homes with less chaos, more connection, and more fun.

It's built on a simple belief:

Choices are not random. They begin inside us.

A body signal becomes a feeling. A feeling becomes a thought. A thought becomes a choice. And repeated choices become the direction of a life.

This project doesn't claim to have all the answers. It's an invitation — for children, parents, educators, and caregivers to pause, to notice, to become curious, and to ask: What is happening inside me right now?

Because every child has strengths and deserves the chance to understand themselves. And perhaps their greatest superpower is learning they can influence what happens next.

If this project helps even one child feel seen, understood, or find another path forward — then this work, and every patch in my quilt, will continue to matter.

— Charlie
