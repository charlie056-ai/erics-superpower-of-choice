Calm Down Tools

Sometimes feelings get really BIG. These tools help your body feel safer. Tap any one to try it!

😮‍💨The Big Breath
1
Breathe IN slowly — count to 4
2
Hold it — count to 4
3
Breathe OUT slowly — count to 4
4
Do it 3 times. Feel your body slow down.
👀Look Around — 5 Things
1
Find 5 things you can SEE
2
Find 4 things you can TOUCH
3
Find 3 things you can HEAR
4
This pulls your brain back to right now.
🚶Move Your Body
1
Do 10 jumping jacks
2
Shake your whole body!
3
Take a short walk if you can
4
Moving your body moves the big feeling out.

Real Life Practice

These are real situations. Pick one and practice what you would do!

⭐Practice
What happened
You go outside for recess. Your friends are playing a game. You run over but they say the teams are full. Your stomach feels heavy.

What do you do next?

🧰 Your tool
When you feel left out — name it first. Then make a choice. Asking calmly or finding something else both keep your day from getting worse.
⭐Practice
What happened
You are playing a game and you lose. Your face gets hot. You want to flip the board.

What do you do next?

🧰 Your tool
Losing feels awful. Taking a breath or walking away gives your feelings somewhere to go without making things worse.
⭐Practice
What happened
You accidentally knocked over your friend's water bottle. It spilled all over their drawing. They look really upset.

What do you do next?

🧰 Your tool
Mistakes happen. Saying sorry AND helping fix it shows real courage. That is repair.
⭐Practice
What happened
A kid at school says something really mean. Your face gets hot and your eyes start to water.

What do you do next?

🧰 Your tool
Mean words hurt. Saying that was not okay and walking away takes their power. Telling a trusted adult means you are not carrying it alone.

Your feelings are not the problem.

Every feeling you have makes sense. Feelings are not weakness. They are information.

"The problem is never the feeling. The problem is when the feeling makes the choice instead of you."
⭐Teen Mode
⚡

Four steps. Any situation. Every time.

Body → Feeling → Need → Choice
1
Body
Something happens. Your body reacts first — chest tight, face hot, heart racing. Notice it.
2
Feeling
Name what you feel. Angry. Embarrassed. Scared. Naming it gives you distance from it.
3
Need
Under every feeling is a need. What do you actually need right now?
4
Choice
This is your superpower. What happens next is always your choice.
"You will not always get it right. But you can always choose better than last time."

Real Situations. Real Practice.

⭐Teen Scenarios
The situation
A close friend has gone silent. No texts back. But they are posting on social media like everything is fine. You feel confused and hurt.

What do you do?

🧰 Your tool
Being ghosted hurts. One direct honest message is fair — then the ball is in their court. You cannot control their behavior. You can control yours.
⭐Teen Scenarios
The situation
You just got home after a rough day. Your parent immediately starts asking about homework and chores. You snap — Can you just leave me alone?

What do you do next?

🧰 Your tool
Your need for space was real. How it came out was hard. Waiting until calm and explaining what you needed keeps the door open.
⭐Teen Scenarios
The situation
Everything is piling up at once. School. Family tension. A friendship that feels off. You are not sleeping well. You feel like you cannot keep up.

What feels most true for you right now?

🧰 Your tool
When everything feels too big — tell one person even a little of what is going on. And do one small thing you can finish today. Both remind your brain that you are not stuck.

⭐Parent Mode
🧠

Your child is not giving you a hard time. They are having a hard time.

"Behavior is always communication. The question is — what is it trying to say?"
Children act out because they are missing words.
Their behavior IS their language. It is the only tool they have right now.
You are not failing. You are overwhelmed.
When you are exhausted and stretched thin your patience runs out fast. That is human — not a character flaw.
The goal is a common language — not a perfect child.
This framework helps you and your child find a middle ground — a way to hear each other before things explode.
⭐Parent Mode
💬

Closes the door
"Why are you being so difficult?"
Opens the door
"Help me understand — what is hard right now?"
Closes the door
"Calm down right now."

"You should know better by now."

"A child who feels connected wants to try harder for you."
⭐Parent Scenarios

Real Family Situations.

⭐Parent Scenarios
The situation
Your child has been home for an hour. Homework is not started. They are on their device. You have asked twice. The third time they say I will do it later.

What do you do?

🧰 Your tool
Resistance to homework is often about overwhelm — not defiance. Asking what is making it hard or helping with the first small step works better than taking things away.
⭐Parent Scenarios
The situation
Your teen comes home and goes straight to their room. They are clearly upset. When you ask if they are okay they say fine and close the door.

What do you do?

🧰 Your tool
Teens often need to process alone before they can connect. Staying available without pressure keeps the door open without forcing it.
⭐Parent Scenarios
The situation
You found out your child lied to you. They looked you in the eyes and said something that was not true. You feel hurt and betrayed on top of angry.

What do you do first?

🧰 Your tool
Children often lie because the truth feels too risky. Addressing both the lie AND the reason it happened calmly teaches honesty better than punishment alone.
⭐Parent Mode
🔧

Repair is possible. Always.

"When you go back and repair — you teach your child something they will carry for life."
1
Wait until you are both calm.
Repair cannot happen in the middle of the storm.
2
Name what happened honestly.
"I got really frustrated and I said something that was not fair to you. I am sorry."
3
Listen to their experience.
"How did that feel for you?" Then just listen. Do not defend.
4
Reconnect.
"I love you. We are okay. Let us try again."

⭐Educator Mode
🧠

The behavior is not the problem.

When a student acts out — that behavior is a message. It is the only language they have right now.

"Stop asking what is wrong with this student. Start asking what this student is trying to tell me."
The four needs most often underneath difficult behavior:
Control — Fairness — Connection — Safety

When a student feels any of these are missing — their behavior tells you before their words do.
⭐Educator Mode
🧰

The Missing Tool Principle.

When a student keeps struggling — ask a different question.

Instead of asking what is wrong with this student
Ask: What tool might be missing?
"A plumber with one wrench is not a bad plumber. They just need more tools for the job. Students are the same."

⭐Educator Scenarios

Situations You Know Well.

⭐Educator Scenarios
The situation
Mid-lesson a student suddenly pushes their chair back loudly says this is stupid and puts their head down. The class goes quiet. Every eye is on you.

What do you do first?

🧰 Your tool
Public correction usually escalates. Giving the class a task and checking in quietly removes the audience and gives you real information about what happened.
⭐Educator Scenarios
The situation
A student who used to participate has gone quiet over the past two weeks. Nothing dramatic has happened but something has clearly shifted.

What do you do?

🧰 Your tool
A student who withdraws is often waiting to see if anyone notices. A private note or quiet check in says I see you without putting them on the spot.
⭐Educator Scenarios
The situation
You have the same student in your office for the third time this month. Same behavior. Same conversation. Same outcome. You are running out of ideas.

What do you shift in your approach?

🧰 Your tool
When the same conversation produces the same result — the conversation needs to change. Asking what tool is missing or asking the student directly often breaks the cycle.
